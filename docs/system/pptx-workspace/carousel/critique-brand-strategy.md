## 1. HOOK ANALYSIS (Slide 1)

**Current:**
> I KEPT STARTING EVERY AI SESSION FROM SCRATCH
> So I built a system where each one gets smarter.

**Verdict: 5/10.** It's fine. It's not wrong. But "fine" doesn't stop a scroll.

### Problems

1. **Too slow to name the domain.** "Every AI session" is vague. Which AI? ChatGPT? Copilot? Cursor? Claude Code? The reader has to get to slide 3 before they know this is a Claude Code thing. That's two slides of borrowed attention you might not get.

2. **"From scratch" is a cliche.** Developers have seen this framing a hundred times. "I was doing X manually, so I automated it." It reads like every indie hacker launch tweet from 2024.

3. **The subline does the real work, but it's quiet.** "Each one gets smarter" is the interesting claim. It's buried as a subline. That's backwards.

4. **Jack's instinct is right — this doesn't sound like a builder.** "I kept starting every AI session from scratch" reads like copywriter-optimized pain-point language. A builder would say something sharper, weirder, more specific.

### 5 Alternative Hooks (Ranked by Predicted Engagement)

**1. (Bold claim — highest predicted engagement)**
> MY AI AGENT REMEMBERS WHAT IT LEARNED LAST WEEK
> I open-sourced the system that makes it happen.

Why it works: Specific, slightly unbelievable claim. Forces a "wait, how?" reaction. Names the output (open-source). Feels like a builder flexing, not a marketer pitching.

**2. (Pain-first, developer-specific)**
> CLAUDE CODE FORGETS EVERYTHING BETWEEN SESSIONS
> So I built 14 commands to fix that.

Why it works: Names the tool in 2 words. "14 commands" is concrete and swipeable. The audience self-selects immediately: Claude Code users lean in, everyone else keeps scrolling (which is what you want — qualified attention beats vanity impressions).

**3. (Curiosity gap)**
> I ADDED 12 AGENTS TO CLAUDE CODE
> Here's what my workflow looks like now.

Why it works: "12 agents" is a number that makes people go "what are they?" The second line promises a reveal. It's a peek-behind-the-curtain hook, which plays well for builder audiences.

**4. (Result-first)**
> MY AI SESSIONS COMPOUND NOW
> Each one starts where the last one left off.

Why it works: "Compound" is a loaded word for the LinkedIn audience — it triggers the mental model of exponential returns. "Starts where the last one left off" is the payoff framed simply.

**5. (Provocative question)**
> WHY DOES YOUR AI START FROM ZERO EVERY TIME?
> Mine doesn't. Here's the system.

Why it works: Direct challenge. The "mine doesn't" is a mic-drop beat that creates envy. Risk: could feel slightly aggressive, but for a developer audience, directness plays well.

## 2. PROBLEM SLIDE (Slide 2)

**Current:**
> SOUND FAMILIAR?
> - Context amnesia / You re-explain your project every session
> - Broken continuity / Features break because context is lost
> - No compounding / Every session feels like day one

### Problems

1. **"Sound familiar?" is dead weight.** It's the LinkedIn equivalent of "So, yeah." It doesn't advance the argument. It doesn't name a category. It's a filler headline that says "I couldn't think of something better." Delete it.

2. **The three pain points are actually good — but they all say the same thing.** Context amnesia, broken continuity, and no compounding are three symptoms of one problem: sessions don't persist. The reader might think "aren't these all the same?" You need either (a) sharper differentiation between them, or (b) reframe them as a progression: what you lose, what breaks, and what never gets built.

3. **The descriptions are too abstract.** "You re-explain your project every session" — to whom? In what tool? "Features break because context is lost" — what kind of features? Get specific. The more the reader thinks "that happened to me yesterday," the more they engage.

### 3 Alternative Problem Framings

**1. (Name the problem category — recommended)**
> THREE THINGS AI CAN'T DO YET
>
> - **Remember** — Context dies when the session ends
> - **Continue** — Yesterday's feature is today's mystery
> - **Compound** — Session 50 is no smarter than session 1

Why: This frames Project Starter as solving fundamental limitations of AI, not just user inconvenience. It elevates the tool. The verb-based card titles (Remember, Continue, Compound) are scannable and memorable.

**2. (Progression framing)**
> THE REAL COST OF STARTING OVER
>
> - **Monday:** You explain your stack, your patterns, your constraints
> - **Tuesday:** You explain them again
> - **Friday:** Your agent breaks the feature it built on Monday

Why: Narrative structure. The reader sees their own week. It's concrete rather than abstract. "Breaks the feature it built on Monday" is a gut-punch moment of recognition.

**3. (Numbers framing)**
> THE FIRST 20 MINUTES OF EVERY SESSION ARE WASTED
>
> - Re-explaining project structure
> - Re-stating coding conventions
> - Re-discovering what was built yesterday

Why: A specific (if invented) number anchors the cost. The "re-" repetition hammers the redundancy. It's visceral. Caveat: only use a number you can defend if challenged in comments.

## 4. OVERALL VIRAL POTENTIAL

### Biggest Weakness

**The carousel doesn't have a "screenshot moment."** The single biggest driver of LinkedIn carousel shares is one slide that's so good, so specific, so useful that people screenshot it or reference it in comments. Right now, no slide does that. Slide 4 (the pipeline) comes closest, but it's explanatory, not revelatory. You need one slide that makes someone think: "I need to save this."

### Single Highest-Impact Change

**Rewrite the hook to name Claude Code and make a specific, bold claim.** The hook determines whether 80% of your potential audience ever sees slide 2. Right now it's too generic to filter for the right audience and too soft to stop the wrong one. Hook #2 from my alternatives ("Claude Code forgets everything between sessions / So I built 14 commands to fix that") would likely double your swipe-through rate because it self-selects and creates immediate curiosity.

### Tone Check

The tone is 70% right. It reads as builder-sharing-work, which is correct. But it occasionally drifts into "product launch" territory — especially on Slide 3 (stats line) and Slide 5 (benefit cards). The fix is small: add one moment of honest imperfection. Something like "It's opinionated. It won't fit every workflow." Vulnerability and specificity build trust faster than polish.

The CTA slide nails the tone: "Fork it. Steal it. Make it yours." That's authentic builder energy. More of that throughout.

### CTA Critique (vs. Best Practices)

Your CTA research says the highest-performing pattern is **hybrid: brand + question**. Your current CTA has:
- Brand element: None (no photo, no @jackrich7, no identity)
- Question/comment driver: None
- Action: Implicit (visit the repo)

"There's no one right way to build with AI" is a pleasant thought that does zero work. It doesn't drive a comment, a save, or a share. It's a bumper sticker on the way out the door.

**Recommended CTA rewrite:**
> FORK IT. STEAL IT. MAKE IT YOURS.
>
> github.com/jackrich78/project-starter
> MIT License
>
> What's your biggest frustration with AI session continuity?
> — @jackrich7

The question drives comments. The handle drives follows. The repo link drives clicks. Three mechanisms instead of one.
